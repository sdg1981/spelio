# Spelio Welsh Foundations

## Introduction

Welsh spelling is often more predictable than it first appears.
These short lessons introduce some of the most useful Welsh spelling patterns found in everyday Welsh.
Try to notice patterns rather than memorise rules.
Over time, these patterns begin to feel natural.

## Future curriculum note

The current Foundations material remains focused on core Welsh spelling patterns, decoding confidence, and written-form recognition.

Future versions of Spelio may explore context-dependent spelling patterns, for example Welsh mutations, where correct written Welsh depends on neighbouring words. This would be approached from a spelling-confidence perspective rather than as a comprehensive grammar curriculum.

Possible long-term spelling-confidence sequence:

1. Foundations 1 — Core Patterns
2. Foundations 2 — Pattern Extensions
3. Foundations 3 — Advanced Recognition
4. Written Welsh — Context & Mutation Patterns

This is exploratory curriculum thinking, not a committed roadmap or an expansion of the current MVP. Any future mutation work should stay carefully scoped around spelling mechanics, recognition, and written accuracy, not a full Welsh grammar course.

# Y

## Primer

The letter Y

🔊 Y (final syllable)

🔊 Y (earlier syllable)

The letter Y can make different sounds in Welsh.
Earlier in a word it often sounds a little like the “u” in the English word “cut”.
In the final syllable of a word — and often in one-syllable words — it more commonly sounds like the “ee” in the English word “beet”.

## Words

### Final syllable / one-syllable examples

* byd — world
* hyd — length
* tŷ — house
* dyn — man

### Earlier syllable examples

* dynes — woman

  * Useful note: Here, Y sounds different because it appears earlier in the word.

* yma — here
* yn — in
* ysgol — school
* ysbyty — hospital

  * Useful note: This word contains Y in two different positions.

## Teaching Context

Y behaves differently depending on where it appears in a word.
This is one of the most useful Welsh spelling patterns to become familiar with.

# W

## Primer

The letter W

🔊 W as a vowel

🔊 W before a vowel

W often behaves like a vowel in Welsh.
It can sound a little like the “oo” in the English word “pool”.
When W comes before a vowel, it often sounds more like the W in the English word “wet”.

## Words

### W as a vowel

* cwm — valley
* bws — bus
* swper — supper
* drws — door
* dŵr — water
* gŵr — husband / man

### W before a vowel

* gwen — white / fair

  * Useful note: Notice how W behaves differently before a vowel.

* gwas — servant / lad
* gwerth — value / worth

## Teaching Context

Unlike English, Welsh often uses W as part of the vowel sound.
This pattern appears throughout the language.
